# Executive Briefing: The Fork That Determines Whether AI Compounds or Stalls

**Source URL**: https://natesnewsletter.substack.com/p/executive-briefing-the-leveling-crisis
**Date**: January 4, 2026
**Publication**: Nate's Newsletter

## Summary

Argues that separate technical and non-technical skill trees are obsolete: the skills that matter when AI handles generation -- specifying intent, maintaining authority over outputs, building compounding workflows, and creating self-improving systems -- are identical across functions. Anchored by Andrej Karpathy's statement that he's "never felt this behind as a programmer" while sensing he could be "10x more powerful," the piece proposes a unified four-level AI problem-solving skill tree applicable across legal, finance, engineering, and operations. The critical fork at the base is tool-mode vs. infrastructure-mode, and leaders who fail to define this tree leave their organizations stuck on skill trees built for a disappearing world.

## Key Claims

- Andrej Karpathy said he's "never felt this behind as a programmer" and senses he could be "10x more powerful" if he figured out how to work with emerging AI systems.
- The skill trees for technical and non-technical roles are merging -- a lawyer building a contract review workflow and an engineer building a debugging assistant are climbing the same tree.
- Four levels of capability apply across all knowledge functions, from legal to finance to engineering.
- The fork at the base of the new skill tree is tool-mode (using AI as a tool) vs. infrastructure-mode (building AI systems).
- The key skills when AI handles generation: specifying intent, maintaining authority over AI outputs, building non-heroic workflows, and creating systems that improve over time.
- This is not a philosophical question but a budget, hiring, and competence-definition question.
- Organizations that fail to define the unified skill tree leave teams stuck on skill trees built for a world that is disappearing.
