# AI and the Human Condition

**Source URL**: https://stratechery.com/2026/ai-and-the-human-condition/
**Date**: January 5, 2026
**Publication**: Stratechery

## Summary

Thompson responds to Dwarkesh Patel and Philip Trammell's "Capital in the 22nd Century" thesis -- that AI will break the labor-capital correction mechanism, concentrating wealth permanently among capital owners. Thompson is skeptical, arguing that humans fundamentally want other humans: for content, for relationships, for beauty. He makes the case that AI-generated abundance will actually increase the value of human provenance, and that new job categories will emerge (as they always have post-automation) that we cannot yet imagine. The inequality concern, he argues, is driven by relative rather than absolute comparisons -- a human nature problem, not an economic one.

## Key Claims

- Patel/Trammell argue that once AI substitutes for labor, capital will compound without the self-correcting mechanism of diminishing returns, leading to extreme inequality.
- In 1810, 81% of the U.S. population worked in agriculture; today only 1% does -- yet entirely new job categories emerged each time.
- Sora was ranked 59th in the App Store, with double-digit human-denominated social apps ranking above it.
- Thompson predicts widespread AI art availability will make human art more desirable and valuable due to provenance.
- Human happiness is relative, not absolute -- Louis C.K.'s "everything is amazing and nobody's happy" observation applied to AI economics.
- Content creators may benefit from AI because shared human-created content provides "totems" for community formation, which individualized AI output cannot.
