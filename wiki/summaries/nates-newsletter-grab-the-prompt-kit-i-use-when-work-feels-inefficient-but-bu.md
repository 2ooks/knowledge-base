# Grab the Prompt Kit I Use When Work Feels Inefficient But Busy

**Source URL**: https://natesnewsletter.substack.com/p/8-habits-worth-unlearning-in-the
**Date**: January 15, 2026
**Publication**: Nate's Newsletter (Substack)

## Summary

An argument that AI has inverted the economics of knowledge work: execution is now cheap, but organizations are still running habits designed to protect expensive execution capacity. The real bottleneck has shifted to clarity (knowing what to build), ambition (swinging big when constraints lift), distribution (reaching customers), and relationships (trust that compounds). The article catalogs seven legacy habits that no longer make sense -- permission loops, polish-as-procrastination, meetings-as-default, structured waiting, over-planning, decks-over-demos, consensus-before-action -- and provides concrete experiments to start breaking them.

## Key Claims

- Cursor went from $1M to $500M ARR faster than any SaaS company in history, driven by making coding less important than judgment about what to build.
- Anthropic's internal research found engineers can fully delegate only about 20% of their work to AI; the rest requires active human judgment.
- AI has inverted the planning-doing ratio: the meeting to discuss a feature now takes longer than building a prototype.
- The four new scarce resources are clarity, ambition, distribution, and relationships -- not execution capacity.
- Cognition (Devin) partnered with Infosys for distribution through 320,000 people rather than building their own, illustrating that distribution is the new moat.
- The shift feels like "compression" -- time compression (5 months feels absurdly padded when 10 days is possible), process compression, and decision compression.
- The article is explicitly not "move fast and break things" -- it requires more judgment, not less, because old rituals partly offloaded judgment onto processes.
