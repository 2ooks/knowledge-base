# The Third Golden Age of Software Engineering -- Thanks to AI, with Grady Booch

**Source URL**: https://newsletter.pragmaticengineer.com/p/the-third-golden-age-of-software
**Date**: February 4, 2026
**Publication**: The Pragmatic Engineer

## Summary

Grady Booch (UML co-creator, IBM Chief Scientist for Software Engineering) frames today's AI disruption as the third in a series of golden ages: algorithms (1940s-1970s), object-oriented abstractions (1970s-2000s), and now systems-level thinking (2000s-present). He directly responds to Dario Amodei's claims about AI replacing software engineers, arguing that current AI tools are trained mostly on patterns already seen and excel at automating known patterns (especially CRUD web systems) but cannot replace systems thinking. For BD, the actionable insight is that infrastructure and delivery pipelines are the "low-hanging fruit" for AI automation -- people in those roles need to re-skill, and tooling vendors targeting CI/CD and DevOps will see fastest adoption.

## Key Claims

- Three golden ages: algorithms (1940s-1970s), OO abstractions (1970s-2000s), systems (2000s-present)
- AI coding tools are "akin to what was happening with compilers" -- a rise in abstraction, not the end of engineering
- Current AI tools are trained mostly on patterns already seen, excelling at web-centric CRUD automation
- Infrastructure and software delivery pipelines are "low hanging fruit" for AI automation and job displacement
- The shift is from programs and apps to systems -- engineers who understand complexity at scale will see greater demand
- Deep foundations become more important as the field accelerates; Minsky's "Society of Mind" recommended for architectural guidance
- Existential crises about automation have recurred with every major abstraction shift (compilers, higher-level languages, etc.)
- Software engineering is an "astonishingly young field" at ~70 years old -- the term "software" wasn't coined until the 1950s
