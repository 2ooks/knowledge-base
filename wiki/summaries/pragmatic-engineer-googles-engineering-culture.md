# Google's Engineering Culture

**Source URL**: https://newsletter.pragmaticengineer.com/p/googles-engineering-culture
**Date**: October 15, 2025
**Publication**: The Pragmatic Engineer

## Summary

A deep-dive based on interviews with 20+ current and former Googlers revealing how Google's engineering culture actually operates: custom internal tooling (Piper monorepo, Borg, Bazel, Cider), design-doc-driven development, the GRAD performance review system, and a culture of continuous rewrites. The episode exposes a key competitive dynamic: Google's heavily customized internal stack creates both engineering advantages (deep integration, tooling quality) and lock-in risks (skills that don't transfer, internal tools that lag behind open-source alternatives). For BD, this maps Google's engineering DNA and its implications for why Google Cloud adoption and open-source strategy play out the way they do.

## Key Claims

- Google stores billions of lines of code in a single monorepo (Piper), one of the largest in the industry
- Google's custom internal tools (Borg, Bazel, Cider, Tricorder) are deeply integrated but create skill portability issues for engineers
- The GRAD performance review system replaced the prior Perf system
- Google has a "readability" process where engineers earn approval to submit code in specific languages
- OKRs originated at Google via John Doerr's influence, shaping a culture of measurable outcomes
- Google has a persistent culture of rewriting and migrating systems, sometimes counterproductively
- On-call setup is considered among the best in the industry, with generous perks for engineers
- Internal transfers and "freedom of movement" are key retention tools
