# Why There Is No "AlphaFold for Materials" -- AI for Materials Discovery with Heather Kulik

**Source URL**: https://www.latent.space/p/materials
**Date**: March 24, 2026
**Publication**: Latent Space

## Summary

MIT's Heather Kulik, a pioneer in combining computational tools with data-driven materials modeling, explains why there cannot be a single "AlphaFold moment" for materials science: unlike proteins (20 amino acids, relatively constrained design space), materials involve vast chemical diversity where each element introduces new interactions with little transferability. The conversation highlights that the best available datasets are noisy DFT approximations, experimental data is scarce and requires interpretation, and LLMs still cannot perform basic domain tasks like designing a 22-heavy-atom ligand reliably. For BD, this signals that AI-for-materials remains a long-horizon investment requiring specialized data infrastructure, not just model scale.

## Key Claims

- AlphaFold works in a constrained space (20 amino acids); materials science has effectively unlimited chemical design space with minimal transferability between elements
- AI-designed polymers tested 4x tougher than expected, discovering a purely quantum mechanical effect that surprised lab collaborators
- LLMs consistently fail at designing a 22-heavy-atom ligand for metal-organic frameworks (MOFs), though they succeed for protein kinases -- suggesting domain-specific reasoning gaps
- The best materials datasets are noisy DFT approximations; "we have really good datasets for really boring chemistry"
- Literature-mined data often has inconsistencies between reported values and paper graphs
- Academic researchers need organized ML-ready data, high-throughput lab access, and compute resources to remain competitive
- Startups raising $100M+ and hyperscaler/pharma AI efforts are reshaping the role of academic scientists
