# The Focus System I Borrowed from an Engineer's Blog + 18 Prompts to Actually Move the Dials

**Source URL**: https://natesnewsletter.substack.com/p/grab-the-18-prompts-i-use-to-diagnose
**Date**: December 8, 2025
**Publication**: Nate's Newsletter

## Summary

Applies an engineering-style three-variable model of focus (interruption rate, recovery time, minimum work block size) from engineer Can Duruk to explain why knowledge workers feel unproductive despite working all day. The article argues AI's role is not generic productivity boosting but specifically nudging these three variables: reducing interruptions via triage systems, cutting context reload time via state capture, and reshaping work to fit fragmented schedules. Includes 18 prompts organized around diagnosis, intervention, and team-level management. For BD relevance, this is a practical framework for how AI copilot tools create measurable value -- not through "do more" but through "protect the conditions for deep work."

## Key Claims

- Knowledge workers switch activities roughly every 3 minutes; receive 7-8 email/IM alerts per hour per field studies.
- Brain recovery after each interruption takes 10-16 minutes, meaning most days produce zero 15-minute blocks of uninterrupted focus at realistic interruption rates.
- Roughly half of workplace interruptions are self-inflicted (tab-switching, inbox checking, Slack grazing).
- A single fewer interruption per hour can dramatically change weekly deep work block count due to cascade effects.
- Microsoft research data suggests heavy collaborators see interruptions every couple of minutes during core hours.
- The three-variable model: lambda (interruption rate per hour), delta (recovery time), theta (minimum block size for meaningful work).
- Duruk's simulation: at realistic parameters, deep work is statistically rare -- not a character flaw, but an environmental design problem.
- AI's highest-leverage focus role: building "reload packets" that cut context-switching recovery time, not generating more work.
