Technological Scale and Government Control
If there was one thing I wish I had changed about yesterday’s Article
Anthropic and Alignment
, it would have been to add what I thought was an obvious caveat: my goal was not to
endorse
what happened between the U.S. government and Anthropic, but rather to
analyze
. I felt so much of the discussion over the weekend was getting mired in the weeds about what exactly Anthropic objected to, or what OpenAI agreed to, that a much more fundamental point — one I’ve been concerned about for a long time now — was getting lost.
Specifically, if AI is going to be as powerful as its advocates say it’s going to be, then it is going to attract the attention of government; moreover, that government is not only going to seek to control AI, but if it can’t, it’s going to seek to destroy it. This isn’t a normative statement about whether this is good or bad (and, as many folks who were confused by my take accurately noted, of course I think that government interference in private companies is usually bad): it’s an analysis that it is. It certainly isn’t very fun to be called a fascist or a bootlicker or what have you for making this point, but I don’t regret it, because I don’t think I’m making a prediction: I think it’s the way to make sense of what happened last week, and if people start taking this reality seriously I think we will end up in a better place.
There is one extra historical point I wanted to make about the nuclear analogy. The American government famously developed the atomic bomb under the auspices of the Manhattan Project; in other words, nuclear power started under government control, and for that reason there has never been any question as to whether or not government has control. This wasn’t an oddity: who else, other than the government, was going to spend an (inflation-adjusted) $36 billion for a military weapon in the 1940s?
Where things changed was with chips and Bob Noyce. While much of Fairchild Semiconductor’s early work was for the military — the only buyers in the earliest days — Noyce increasingly turned down design commissions from the military in the later years of Fairchild and when he left to found Intel. Part of it was that accepting R&D money from the government meant the government got your IP; part of it was that Noyce was convinced that the government market would be dwarfed in size by the civilian market, and, given the nearly zero marginal cost nature of chips, pursuing the latter would not only be far more profitable, but would actually drive far more demand in the long run, thanks to the lower prices that would come from scale, and ultimately result in much more innovation in both computers and chips themselves.
That’s exactly what happened: by the late 1960s the civilian market was buying more chips than the government, and the lion’s share of those chips were from Fairchild; fast forward to the 1980s and the military embarked on a massive modernization program that was predicated on leveraging civilian technology — which was far ahead of the military’s technology — to make an entirely new generation of weapons. For chips, volume and demand mattered far more than the military’s need; that need was actually best met by buying off-the-shelf.
AI has this dynamic on steroids: the cost of training these models is so large that the amount of investment only makes sense if the market is massive, which is why these models are being developed by private companies on clouds owned by private companies using chips made by other private companies. The military — which was the only customer and thus only funder of nuclear weapons in the 1940s — is just another customer of many, which is actually a good thing: by using privately-developed AI the military gets access to much more capable and rapidly advancing AI than they would if they funded it themselves.
The rub, of course, was yesterday’s Article: AI has the potential to be so powerful that, even though the government didn’t fund its development and isn’t the largest customer, it will still demand control of it, or, at a minimum, to have unfettered access to it. This is a lot more complicated than the nuclear question, where government control and ownership was a given; the sort of actions taken by the government over the last week fly in the face of the concept of private decision making and property rights. Unfortunately, those rights are not set in stone: they are ultimately enforced by someone, and the someone who is doing the enforcement is the one doing the violating. That’s not great, but it’s happening and/or will happen, and it’s important to come to grips with that reality — and I don’t think trying to describe that is at all equivalent to endorsing it.
Paramount Outbids Netflix for Warner Bros.
From the
Wall Street Journal
:
Paramount Skydance emerged victorious in the bidding war for Warner Bros. Discovery after Netflix said it wouldn’t match the David Ellison-led company’s latest offer for the iconic Hollywood property. Netflix pulled the plug on its deal soon after the Warner board of directors said it determined Paramount’s $31-per-share offer for the entire company was superior to Netflix’s bid for Warner’s movie and television studios and HBO Max streaming service. Paramount is now poised to take control of the entertainment company, home to properties and brands including HBO, Superman and Harry Potter.
“We’ve always been disciplined, and at the price required to match Paramount Skydance’s latest offer, the deal is no longer financially attractive, so we are declining to match,” Netflix co-Chief Executives Ted Sarandos and Greg Peters said. “This transaction was always a ‘nice to have’ at the right price, not a ‘must have’ at any price,” the pair said.
Pending regulatory approval, Paramount will own not only Warner Bros. and HBO, but also many popular cable networks including CNN, TNT, TBS and Food Network. The deal would represent a ground shift for the entertainment industry, which is trying to adapt to seismic shifts in audience habits and technology.
Sarandos told
Lucas Shaw in an interview on Bloomberg
that Netflix wasn’t concerned about regulatory issues or stock market skepticism of Netflix’s planned acquisition; this was simply a matter of the price being too high.
It’s hard to take that entirely at face value; Netflix has been in the spotlight in Washington like never before as a company, and it’s pretty telling that the stock jumped so significantly upon news that Netflix lost the deal. I don’t buy the narrative that this was all a play to make Paramount pay more than they might have otherwise, but at the same time, I can imagine that there was some amount of relief that accompanied stepping away from this acquisition.
The folks that should be considerably more worried is the rest of Hollywood; Paramount is promising $6 billion in synergies by the end of 2029, which is a fancy M&A word for a massive amount of layoffs. That is certainly ambitious, but it’s also worth pointing out that there will in fact be more synergies than normal because this is very much a horizontal acquisition: Paramount and Warner Bros. are in all the same businesses.
That, by extension, raises the antitrust question. Netflix’s proposed acquisition certainly had some horizontal components — particularly the fact that the leading paid streaming service (excluding YouTube, which you can of course subscribe to) was acquiring a competitor — but it was also fairly vertical in nature: Netflix was a distributor buying a supplier. Per the previous point, however, Warner Bros. and Paramount not only have rival streaming services, but also both have linear cable networks, studios, theatrical arms, etc. This is very much consolidation, not vertical integration, which means it should raise considerably more antitrust scrutiny.
That noted, my initial thought is that the acquisition should be allowed to move forward; consolidation is the natural response in an industry in secular decline. To put it another way, the same caveats that applied to Netflix apply to Paramount and Warner Bros.: the actual competitive space is time and attention broadly, and the biggest threats to both actually stem from the fact that anyone on earth can be a creator.
And frankly, the biggest evidence that this is the real source of pressure was the fact that Netflix attempted this deal in the first place; Netflix has the structural advantage in terms of buying professionally-produced content, but the company felt the need to
pull forward the end game
because, I suspect, it worried about just how much attention there might be left to win whenever it arrives. Now, though, it will have to wait for nature — and Paramount’s debt load — to run its course. To quote from the end of Sarandos’ interview with Shaw:
If you feel like this is a rare asset, that also means that an asset like this may not come up again anytime soon, right?
