The (A)Innovators Dilemma
The talk of the town in AI world has recently been margins. Quietly, however, the hard constraint is still compute. The biggest labs and the largest AI application providers are rationing it every day. They decide how much goes to research vs training vs serving current demand, and which products get priority. Rate limits move around and have to be super dynamic as everyone is compute constrained. Just the skill about being dynamic with your compute is starting to be a competitive advantage…
That rationing has a second order effect. Winners get walled off. If you have a product with real traction, it will soak up an enormous share of your available compute just to keep latency down and reliability high (because at the end of the day, you’ll want to protect that application with real traction). Think about ChatGPT for OpenAI or coding for Anthropic. Each could justify a huge and steady slice of the pie, and the pie is not infinite. The result is a company that wants to do everything, but in practice is optimizing the engine that already works.
Here is where a familiar dilemma (innovators dilemma) shows up in a new way. A fresh vector of growth appears (maybe even one that could challenge the core competency of an existing leader). It is early, unproven, and moving fast. To chase it, you must pull compute from the cash cow that pays the bills and anchors your brand. In classic software, the constraint was people and attention. In AI, the constraint is people, attention, and a
very real pool of scarce hardware
. If you are late (to move towards the new vector of growth) by even a few months you can miss the wave completely. Coding agents are a good example of how fast a category can tip.
This dynamic will create openings for startups. You can build a focused product that does not need to fight an internal compute budget battle every week. You can design for efficiency first, route across providers, and accept a smaller absolute footprint so you can move faster. Incumbents will talk about platform strategy while they wait for another rack to come online. Startups can just ship.
How the rationing shows up inside large AI companies:
Serving vs training vs research: do you favor user experience today or the next frontier of capability tomorrow
Product vs product: do you protect the leader or give the new thing a real shot
Reliability vs reach: do you lower latency and increase quality for current users or open the gates to more users and risk performance
None of these are abstract tradeoffs. They are daily scheduling decisions that shape product roadmaps.
Why can startups win? Same reasons they always can…
You choose a narrow job and shape models, prompts, and data around that job. Lower waste means lower compute per user.
You can arbitrage supply across clouds and regions and accept uneven availability while you grow. Big brands have higher SLO commitments and less flexibility.
You build feedback loops and data moats in a single domain while incumbents defend a general product.
You ship into new waves on day one because there is no internal fight over who loses capacity.
The path is not easy. You still face cost pressure, GPU supply risk, and the need to prove durable unit economics. But you are not trying to turn a cargo ship around…
So what could this mean? What might be the implications of this?
Persistent waitlists or quiet throttling in flagship products
Shifts in price or quality tiers that signal rationing
More partnerships and licensing in categories that look hot
These are all tells that the constraint is biting.
Compute scarcity is turning into the new organizational constraint. It forces big AI companies to favor the proven thing and delay the next thing. That is the modern version of the innovator’s dilemma. The advantage tilts to teams that can make fast and repeated reallocation decisions, or better yet, avoid the internal reallocation battle altogether. Startups that are efficient, narrowly focused, and willing to live with a little mess will grab the next waves while the leaders protect their cash cows.

Update on Multiples
SaaS businesses are generally valued on a multiple of their revenue - in most cases the projected revenue for the next 12 months. Revenue multiples are a shorthand valuation framework. Given most software companies are not profitable, or not generating meaningful FCF, it’s the only metric to compare the entire industry against. Even a DCF is riddled with long term assumptions. The promise of SaaS is that growth in the early years leads to profits in the mature years. Multiples shown below are calculated by taking the Enterprise Value (market cap + debt - cash) / NTM revenue.

Rule of 40 shows rev growth + FCF margin (both LTM and NTM for growth + margins). FCF calculated as Cash Flow from Operations - Capital Expenditures
GM Adjusted Payback is calculated as: (Previous Q S&M) / (Net New ARR in Q x Gross Margin) x 12. It shows the number of months it takes for a SaaS business to pay back its fully burdened CAC on a gross profit basis. Most public companies don’t report net new ARR, so I’m taking an implied ARR metric (quarterly subscription revenue x 4). Net new ARR is simply the ARR of the current quarter, minus the ARR of the previous quarter. Companies that do not disclose subscription rev have been left out of the analysis and are listed as NA.
